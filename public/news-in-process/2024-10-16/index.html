October 16, 2024

The 2024 election has been unusual in many ways. It started as a rematch between Trump and Biden at the top of the major party tickets. There was an initial debate shock forcing Biden to withdraw from the race and endorse his vice president, Kamala Harris, to lead the Democratic ticket for president.

There was no Democratic primary following Biden’s withdrawal. There were no floor fights at the Democratic National Convention. Rather there has been unity among Democrats, even to the point of welcoming Republicans into their fold.

There are political commentators who say that the 2024 presidential election has been a close election for the last 130 days. Our forecasting model tells a different story.

With a much shortened election cycle, we have seen great volatility in polls and prediction markets across the last 130 days.

For the first time in US history, a presidential election has gone from toss-up to Republican landslide, to toss-up, to a possible Democratic landslide, to toss-up, and then to an election that leans Republican.

As we head into the last days before the November election, we expect additional volatility. Democratic partisans are concerned about recent polls and prediction markets. And rightly so.

How does the 2024 race for the White House compare with 2020?

For the 2020 presidential election, The Virtual Tout® employed a bottom-up forecasting approach, updating electoral vote forecasts every hour and posting those forecasts to Twitter.

In the 2024 presidential election, we are using a top-down forecasting approach, as described under Top-Down Forecasting, Focus on Electoral Votes, Estimating Vote Shares, and Electoral Vote Step Functions.

Despite differences in methods, we have been consistent in using PredictIt as a trusted data source. As explain in Prediction Markets, we rely on seven criteria for selecting a prediction market source, and PredictIt satisfies those criteria more fully than other prediction markets.

For the 2020 presidential election, forecasted Democratic electoral votes were high at the beginning of October, following the first presidential debate between Trump and Biden in late September and Donald Trump’s October 2 announcement that he was suffering from Covid-19. Forecasted electoral votes were lowest on the morning of the election, November 3, 2020. The Biden/Harris ticket won the 2020 presidential election with 306 electoral votes.

For Democratic partisans, it may be disconcerting to see that at no time during October and early November of 2020 did the Democratic ticket of Biden and Harris fall below the 270 line for winning in the Electoral College. We cannot say that about the 2024 Democratic ticket of Harris and Walz.

Forecasted electoral votes in 2020 varied across the final days of the race for the White House, but not nearly so much as 2024 forecasted electoral votes have varied.

On October 16, 2024, the story of this election appears to be heading toward a sad ending for Democratic partisans. They fear that we may be electing a man who does not support fundamental concepts from the Constitution of the United States, including the separation of powers, freedom of the press, and the idea that no person is above the law.

Prediction markets look to the future. Prices vary as investors anticipate what will happen in an election. Many citizens have already cast their votes in 2024. What do those votes show? And what can we expect from citizens between now and election day?

As we approach November 5, The Virtual Tout® will do what have been doing throughout this election: Following the data an leading with science.
